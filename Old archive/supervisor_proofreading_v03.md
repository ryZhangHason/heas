# COMPREHENSIVE PROOFREADING: HEAS WSC 2026 v0.3

## ABSTRACT

**1. [Abstract] Imprecise claim about "independently written code"** → PROBLEM: "recompute the same outcome metric across optimization, tournament evaluation, and statistical validation with independently written code" is vague; it does not clearly state whether the code is independently written *by the same author* (which is possible) or whether independence refers to implementation *at the same stage*. The root problem is semantic divergence, not authorship. → SUGGESTED FIX: "This silent aggregation divergence arises when metric computation is implemented independently at each stage (optimization, tournament evaluation, statistical validation), allowing rankings to differ..."

**2. [Abstract] Grammatical: Comma splice and sentence fragment** → PROBLEM: "Three case studies---ecological, enterprise, and a Wolf-Sheep ecological scenario" uses a dash list followed by "and a Wolf-Sheep ecological scenario," which repeats "ecological" and creates a fragment. The enumeration is also inconsistent: two nouns (ecological, enterprise) followed by a full noun phrase. → SUGGESTED FIX: "Three case studies—ecological population management, enterprise regulatory design, and Wolf-Sheep predation dynamics—validate..."

**3. [Abstract] Logical inconsistency: case studies not truly three** → PROBLEM: "Three case studies---ecological, enterprise, and a Wolf-Sheep ecological scenario" conflates §4.1 (Ecological Population Management) and §4.3 (Wolf-Sheep Predation). Both are ecological systems; the Wolf-Sheep case is explicitly introduced in §4.3 as a "mean-field ODE composition vehicle." This is not a third independent domain; it is a second ecological study. The abstract claims three domains but delivers two (ecology, enterprise). → SUGGESTED FIX: "Two case studies—ecological population management and enterprise regulatory design—validate composability across domains, with a Wolf-Sheep predation model serving as a mean-field ODE vehicle."

**4. [Abstract] Empirical claim unsubstantiated at time of submission** → PROBLEM: "In the tested ecological scenarios, the evolved champion wins all 32 held-out and all 24 single-anchor OOD cases." This is stated as fact, but §5.7 (where this is discussed) reads as a preliminary report. The abstract should not claim results that are not yet finalized. → SUGGESTED FIX: Either move this claim to the conclusion or soften: "Preliminary results from the tested ecological scenarios show the evolved champion winning all 32 held-out OOD cases and all 24 single-anchor cases."

**5. [Abstract] Missing clarity on "metric contracts"** → PROBLEM: The abstract defines metric contracts as "a runtime-enforceable design pattern" but does not concisely explain what they are or why they matter. A reader unfamiliar with the paper may not grasp the core contribution. → SUGGESTED FIX: "HEAS enforces a single metrics_episode() contract—a uniform interface consumed identically by optimization, tournament evaluation, and statistical inference—making divergence provably impossible by construction."

Each step reads the same welfare metric, yet in Mesa each is an independent code path: the DEAP fitness function extracts welfare from the model's DataCollector after each run; the tournament scorer extracts welfare from a separate episode-evaluation function, possibly with different aggregation; and the bootstrap CI loop extracts welfare from a third data collection loop, potentially inconsistent with both." This is verbatim from the Abstract. Contributions should not open with a verbatim restatement of the abstract. → SUGGESTED FIX: Delete this sentence and begin directly with the contribution: "This paper identifies silent aggregation divergence as a structural risk in loosely-coupled ABM pipelines and proposes metric contracts as a composable design pattern for eliminating it."

**12. [§1.2] Imprecise scope claim: "by construction, not post-hoc validation"** → PROBLEM: This phrase appears in the first contribution bullet and again in the third, suggesting either emphasis or redundancy. It is not immediately clear what "post-hoc validation" would entail. → SUGGESTED FIX: Use once, in the most critical location (the opening of the contributions), and clarify: "by construction (signature-checked at dispatch time), not by post-hoc data reconciliation."

**13. [§1.2] Circular definition: "Semantic correctness—whether metrics_episode() correctly captures domain intent—remains the modeller's responsibility, not the framework's."** → PROBLEM: This statement is correct but creates an apparent gap in the contribution: if HEAS only enforces structural consistency and not semantic validity, what prevents an incorrect metrics_episode() from propagating the wrong result consistently through all three stages? The paper should address this explicitly. → SUGGESTED FIX: Add a sentence: "Thus, HEAS prevents structural inconsistency (different aggregation logic at each stage) but requires the modeller to validate that metrics_episode() correctly implements domain logic; incorrect but consistent implementations remain the modeller's responsibility."

**14. **15. It is unclear whether this means pedagogical value, a reusable template, or something else. → SUGGESTED FIX: "This combination provides both a replicable methodological framework and an open-source platform for simulation-based policy search."

**16. [§1.2] Unquantified claim: "validated reference implementation"** → PROBLEM: The phrase "validated reference implementation" suggests extensive validation, but the paper qualifies this heavily in §5.10 (Threats to Validity). At this point in the paper, the validation is not yet presented; forward-referencing a result before it is shown is weak. → SUGGESTED FIX: Simply say "implementation with empirical evidence" and allow §5 to validate the scope.

## § 3 THE HEAS FRAMEWORK

**23. [§3, intro] Missing detail: "SciPy for statistical utilities"** → PROBLEM: The framework description mentions SciPy but never specifies which functions are used. When §3.4 lists statistical utilities, SciPy is not mentioned again. This creates an inconsistency in the dependency discussion. → SUGGESTED FIX: Either specify which SciPy functions are used (e.g., "for Wilcoxon rank-sum tests and BCa bootstrap"), or remove SciPy from the intro and mention it in §3.4.

**24. [§3.1] Vague definition: "Layer with four methods (step, metrics_step, metrics_episode, reset)"** → PROBLEM: The four methods are listed without explanation of what each does or why four are necessary. For instance, why separate metrics_step from metrics_episode? → SUGGESTED FIX: "Layer with four methods: step() (advance one time unit); metrics_step() (return per-step aggregates); metrics_episode() (return per-episode summary dict[str, float]); reset() (reinitialize state)."

**25. [§3.1] Undefined term: "Streams"** → PROBLEM: "Layers compose into Streams---named groups forming a coherent sub-simulation---and Streams compose into an Arena, which orchestrates step ordering, inter-stream signal routing, and episode-level aggregation." The term "named groups forming a coherent sub-simulation" is vague. What makes a group "coherent"? How is this defined formally? → SUGGESTED FIX: "Layers compose into Streams (named collections of Layers corresponding to a single sub-system, e.g., prey dynamics), and Streams compose into an Arena, which orchestrates execution order, inter-stream messaging, and episode-level aggregation."

**26. [§3.1] Unclear naming convention** → PROBLEM: "The naming convention stream_name.metric_name ensures globally unambiguous keys regardless of Arena size." This is stated as a design choice, but the underlying rationale—why global unambiguity matters—is not explained. Without understanding the risk (e.g., metric collisions in large models), this reads as over-specified. → SUGGESTED FIX: "To avoid metric name collisions in large models, the naming convention stream_name.metric_name ensures globally unambiguous keys, allowing metrics_episode() dicts to aggregate safely across Streams."

**27. [§3.2] Vague claim: "typically 5--10 lines"** → PROBLEM: "A researcher adds NSGA-II to a new simulation by writing only the objective function (typically 5--10 lines reading from run_many() results)." This claim is supported only by examples in the case studies, not by empirical measurement. The paper later claims 5 LOC total coupling code (§5.5), so a single objective function at 5--10 lines seems inconsistent. → SUGGESTED FIX: Either provide an example inline or soften: "typically 5--10 lines of objective function code."

**28. [§3.2] Incomplete specification: "per-episode seeds derived deterministically"** → PROBLEM: "run_many() executes episodes in parallel via ProcessPoolExecutor, with per-episode seeds derived deterministically for reproducibility." What is the derivation mechanism? How is determinism guaranteed across parallel processes? → SUGGESTED FIX: "run_many() executes episodes in parallel via ProcessPoolExecutor, seeding each episode deterministically from an initial seed via a counter (episode_id + base_seed), ensuring reproducibility without inter-process synchronization."

**29. [§3.3] Ambiguous phrasing: "Comparing candidate policies is formalized as a Tournament"** → PROBLEM: "Comparing candidate policies is formalized as a Tournament: a cross-product of participants and scenarios evaluated for n_ep episodes and aggregated by argmax, majority, Borda, or Copeland rules." It is not clear whether "cross-product" means (policies × scenarios) or something else. Does each policy compete against all others in all scenarios? → SUGGESTED FIX: "...a cross-product of (policies × scenarios) evaluated independently for n_ep episodes each, then aggregated using..."

**30. [§3.3] Unexplained justification: "The rules are intended to reveal whether rankings are robust to aggregation choice."** → PROBLEM: "The rules are intended to reveal whether rankings are robust to aggregation choice. Universal agreement (as in Section 5.4) indicates clear dominance; disagreement diagnoses the competitive structure." This is a methodological claim, but the paper provides no theoretical or empirical justification for why these four rules specifically are sufficient or why they diagnose competitive structure. → SUGGESTED FIX: Either cite a source defending this choice or note: "These rules represent different consensus criteria: argmax (strongest candidate), majority (plurality consensus), Borda (ranked preference aggregation), and Copeland (pairwise dominance). Disagreement between them indicates that policy rankings are sensitive to the choice of aggregation rule."

**31. [§3.4] Inconsistent utility specifications** → PROBLEM: "All utilities accept list[float] and return structured dicts, requiring no configuration." But the statistical utilities mentioned are summarize_runs(), wilcoxon_test(), cohens_d(), and kendall_tau() (not per-float), while hypervolume() requires a reference point. The claim "no configuration" is contradicted by auto_reference_point(), which is mentioned but not explained. → SUGGESTED FIX: "All utilities accept lists and return structured dicts. auto_reference_point() automatically derives a reference point from data, eliminating manual configuration for hypervolume() computation."

**32. [§3.5, opening] Vague framing: "Minimalism enables composition"** → PROBLEM: "Minimalism enables composition. A richer contract—e.g., dict[str, list[float]] permitting per-step time series, or dict[str, dict[str, float]] enabling agent-level distributions—would require negotiation between model internals and the optimization, tournament, and inference layers." The claim that a richer contract requires "negotiation" is metaphorical and imprecise. What does "negotiation" mean technically? → SUGGESTED FIX: "...would require each framework stage (optimizer, tournament scorer, inference pipeline) to implement separate aggregation logic, reintroducing the coupling-code problem HEAS targets."

**33. [§3.5, loss discussion] Incomplete argument** → PROBLEM: "What is lost. The scalar contract does not directly represent time-series outputs or agent-level heterogeneity (e.g., the distribution of firm sizes, not just mean firm profit). The contract enforces consistent aggregation of agent data rather than removing it: each model retains full internal state, and metrics_episode() may compute any aggregate statistic before returning the scalar summary. For policy-ranking purposes---the intended use case---this loss is bounded: selecting among regulatory regimes requires aggregate summaries as the basis for comparison, even if within-regime agent-level structure remains unmeasured." This section correctly notes the limitation but the justification ("for policy-ranking purposes") is specific to the case studies, not universal. The paper should acknowledge that some applications (e.g., studying inequality across agent populations) require richer metrics. → SUGGESTED FIX: Add: "However, applications requiring detailed agent-level analysis (e.g., studying inequality across firm populations) would require either relaxing the scalar contract or implementing custom aggregation logic within metrics_episode()."

**34. [§3.5, contract enforcement] Confusing clause: "If any stage attempts to access episode metrics via an unregistered function, the signature check raises a ContractViolationError"** → PROBLEM: No ContractViolationError is mentioned elsewhere in the paper. This error type is introduced here without prior definition, and it is unclear how a "signature check" prevents divergence if all three stages can still compute differently as long as they call the same function. → SUGGESTED FIX: Clarify: "All metric access within the framework is routed through a single metrics_episode() implementation. If optimization, tournament, or inference code attempts to compute metrics via any other function, a runtime signature validation raises ContractViolationError, catching divergence at development time."

**35. [§3.5, NSGA-II justification] Unsubstantiated claim: "established performance on policy-search benchmarks"** → PROBLEM: "NSGA-II as reference implementation. Section 3.2 adopts NSGA-II as the default multi-objective evolutionary algorithm based on three criteria: (1) established performance on policy-search benchmarks in the simulation optimization literature; (2) well-understood parametrization at WSC; and (3) ease of replacement via the pluggable run_ea() interface." Criterion (1) lacks a citation. → SUGGESTED FIX: Provide a reference or soften: "NSGA-II is adopted as the reference implementation based on: (1) widespread use in simulation optimization; (2) well-documented parametrization; and (3) ease of replacement via the pluggable run_ea() interface."

**36. [§3.5, graphical interface] Isolated mention: "HEAS ships with a graphical user interface built on the Pydido application framework"** → PROBLEM: This paragraph is isolated and does not connect to the preceding discussion of contract enforcement or the case studies. ## § 5 EVALUATION

**43. [§5.1, opening] Confusion about "structural risk"** → PROBLEM: "We test the hypothesis that silent aggregation divergence is a structural risk---persistent regardless of stochasticity parameter τ---and that metric contracts eliminate it by construction on contract-verified execution paths." The definition of "structural" is not clear. Does it mean "independent of implementation details"? "Inherent in the architecture"? τ is not defined. → SUGGESTED FIX: "We test two hypotheses: (1) silent aggregation divergence is a structural risk—arising from the architecture of loosely-coupled pipelines, not from stochasticity—and (2) metric contracts eliminate it by enforcing a single implementation consumed by all pipeline stages."

**44. [§5.1] Undefined variable: τ** → PROBLEM: "τ is the stochasticity parameter" is never explicitly defined in the paper. RQ1 references "the full τ-range" and "stochasticity parameter τ," but τ is not connected to any model parameter until §5.2 mentions a "τ-sweep." What does τ represent? → SUGGESTED FIX: Define in §5.1: "Let τ ∈ [0, 1] denote the per-step stochasticity coefficient applied to all stochastic draws in the model (e.g., ecological noise, demand variance). τ=0 is deterministic; τ=1 is full stochasticity."

**45. [§5.1] Circular definition of RQ2** → PROBLEM: "RQ2: Does the metric contract itself---not policy quality---drive OOD consistency?" What does "drive OOD consistency" mean? This is vague. Additionally, RQ2 is tested via "a policy-frozen ablation isolating metric implementation (§5.3)," but §5.3 is titled "METRIC CONTRACT CAUSAL ISOLATION: OOD ABLATION" with results marked "[RQ2 --- Results pending; final submission will include ablation data.]" This means RQ2 is not yet answered. → SUGGESTED FIX: Either define RQ2 more precisely ("Does enforcing a single metrics_episode() contract maintain ranking consistency across OOD scenarios, independent of policy quality?") or remove it until results are available.

**46. [§5.1] Missing definition: "Bonferroni correction"** → PROBLEM: "Multiple comparisons apply Bonferroni correction." Bonferroni is mentioned as a standard but its threshold is not pre-specified. Later, §5.8 applies "α=0.05/9=0.0056" but this is ad hoc. Bonferroni is correctly applied, but the paper should state the family-wise error rate (FWER) target upfront. → SUGGESTED FIX: "Multiple comparisons apply Bonferroni correction with family-wise error rate α=0.05."

## § 5.4 CONTROLLED AGGREGATION EXPERIMENT

**49. [§5.4, setup] Confusing contrast: "three fragmented implementations"** → PROBLEM: "We run 15 independent NSGA-II search processes at small scale (150 steps, pop=20, ngen=10) on the ecological case study, extract the Pareto front, and score each candidate across 8 scenarios using four voting rules (argmax, majority, Borda, Copeland). Rank reversals---cases where the tournament winner differs under uniform vs. ad-hoc aggregation---measure contract efficacy." The phrase "three fragmented implementations" refers to "(A) single metrics_episode() throughout vs. (B) three fragmented implementations across 20--30 held-out OOD scenarios" but (B) is not clearly described in §5.4. It is described in §5.3, which has no results. This is a forward-reference to missing content. → SUGGESTED FIX: Fully define both conditions in §5.4: "(A) Uniform contract (HEAS): Single metrics_episode() dict consumed identically by optimization, tournament, and statistics. (B) Ad-hoc aggregation: EA extracts final-step biomass; tournament reads episode-mean biomass; statistics read rolling 10-step mean biomass."

**50. [§5.4, results table] Critical error in interpretation** → PROBLEM: "[TABLE 2: Condition / Rank Reversals / Voting Agreement / Pareto τ — HEAS=0%, Ad-hoc-Step=6.7%, Ad-hoc-Mean=6.7%]" The table caption is vague and the results interpretation is problematic. The text states: "Effect size (HEAS vs. ad-hoc): Cohen's h = 0.476 [95% CI: 0.211--0.680], p < 0.05 (binomial test, two-sided). The uniform metric contract eliminates rank reversals entirely (0%), while ad-hoc-Step aggregation produces reversals in 6.7% of runs (Δ = 6.7%, 95% CI [1.2%, 29.8%])." But 6.7% of 15 runs is 1 run, and 6.7% of 8 scenarios × 4 voting rules × 15 runs is ~40 tests; the CI [1.2%, 29.8%] is inconsistent with 1 reversal in ~40 observations. This suggests confusion about the unit of analysis. → SUGGESTED FIX: Clearly state: "Across 15 runs, 8 scenarios, and 4 voting rules (480 policy evaluations), HEAS produced 0 rank reversals (0/480), while ad-hoc-Step produced reversals in [exact count] evaluations (X%, 95% CI [L%, U%])."

**51. [§5.4, Stage 2 results] Contradictory evidence** → PROBLEM: "In Stage 2 (stochastic robustness test), we re-evaluated the same policies under a different random seed: HEAS maintained perfect ranking consistency (τ = 1.000), while both ad-hoc conditions also showed τ = 1.000, confirming that the deterministic ranking consistency emerges from the metric implementation structure rather than external validation." This conclusion is backwards: if both HEAS and ad-hoc-Step achieve τ=1.000 in the re-evaluation, this suggests that Kendall's τ is not a sensitive measure of aggregation divergence. The fact that rank reversals appeared in Stage 1 but vanished in Stage 2 suggests the divergence is not "structural" (as claimed in §5.1) but rather stochastic or seed-dependent. This undermines RQ1. → SUGGESTED FIX: Revise: "In Stage 2, using different seeds, both HEAS and ad-hoc conditions achieved τ=1.000, suggesting that divergence observed in Stage 1 is seed-dependent rather than structural.
